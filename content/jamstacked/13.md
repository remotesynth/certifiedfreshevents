<div class="intro" style="margin: -5px 5px 15px;">

  <p style="margin-top: 0.8em; margin-bottom: 0.8em;">This week, the co-founder of Wordpress came out with public criticism of the Jamstack. In response, I'd like to say that I think there are really strong reasons to pick Wordpress from the simple onboarding experience to an excellent editing environment, particularly for non-developers. Mullenweg's brief comments touch on these for sure.</p>

<p style="margin-top: 0.8em; margin-bottom: 0.8em;">But Wordpress has its issues, from plugin bloat, to a tightly-coupled frontend and backend, to security issues often tied to not keeping up with the frequent updates (<em>to the core, to plugins, to PHP, etc</em>). These are things Mullenweg did not discuss and some of the reasons that drove many of us to choose Jamstack based upon our own priorities. Coincidentally, Phil Hawksworth of Netlify wrote a post on <a href="https://opensource.com/article/20/9/jamstack" style="text-decoration: none; color: #0088cc; font-weight: 600;">why Jamstack is changing web development</a> that ends up being a good counterpoint to Mullenweg's comments.</p>
  
  <p style="margin-top: 0.8em; margin-bottom: 0.8em;">In the end, it's not a zero sum game. There's room for Jamstack and Wordpress - or even Jamstack + Wordpress, which is why I found the comments needlessly disappointing.</p>
